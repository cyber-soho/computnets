Lesson 1: Physical Cabling

This section explores the physical foundation of the network. Cabling is a 10-year commitment, and poor choices here are extremely costly to fix later. Here, you will interact with data comparing copper and fiber capabilities, and review the strict physical laws (distance, bend radius, interference) that dictate a reliable network installation.

Copper Cable Capabilities

Rule of thumb for 2026: New office = Cat 6a minimum. It guarantees 10 Gbps across the full 100m standard length.

The Laws of Physics

📏

The 100-Meter Rule

TIA-568 limit is 100m total for copper (90m horizontal + 10m patches). Cable path ≈ straight-line × 1.5. Violating it causes silent drops under load.

↩️

Bend Radius

Minimum bend radius for Cat 6a UTP is 4× cable diameter. Tighter bends deform twist geometry, increasing crosstalk and dropping speed. Avoid staples and zip-ties.

⚡

Separation from Power/EMI

Keep data cables at least 30 cm from parallel AC mains. Cross at 90° when necessary. Beware of fluorescent ballasts and old HVAC motors.

✨

Fiber Optic (Glass, not Copper)

Multi-Mode Fiber (MMF)

Distance: 300m – 2km

Optics Cost: Lower

Use Case: Inside building, campus backbone.

Single-Mode Fiber (SMF)

Distance: 10+ km

Optics Cost: Higher

Use Case: Building-to-building, ISP links.

Fiber wins because it is immune to EMI, offers huge distance over a single run, and provides future-proof bandwidth.

The €300 Decision That Saves €3,000

Always buy a Managed switch over an unmanaged one. For approximately €300 more, you gain:

  • Visibility into every port
  • VLAN separation for security
  • QoS (Quality of Service) for voice traffic
  • Alerts when cables go bad
  • 10-minute remote troubleshooting vs. a full day of unplugging things

Lesson 3: Scaling

This section explores how to build a network that grows painlessly. Scaling is a design decision made on day one, not a rebuild project executed in year three. Interact with the chart below to see how Variable Length Subnet Masking (VLSM) efficiently organizes a network compared to a flat topology, and review the four pillars of scalability.

Pillar 1: VLSM Address Plan

A single flat /24 subnet limits you to 254 devices and lacks security. VLSM segments the network efficiently based on purpose.

The 4 Pillars of Scaling

1. IP Planning

Use VLSM. Think in segments, not a single block.

2. Redundancy

Eliminate cheap SPOFs (Dual ISP, HSRP/VRRP, Stacked switches).

3. Monitoring

SNMP, Syslog, NetFlow. A network no one watches fails silently.

4. Documentation

Diagrams, IP plan, cable schedule, change log. (The Bus Test).

Key Takeaway

"Design for the network that will exist in 3 years, not the one that exists today."

VLSM, redundant gateways, centralized monitoring, and honest documentation are not luxuries. They are the difference between a network that scales and one that needs to be rebuilt.
