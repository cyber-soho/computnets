Terminology

Key Concepts — Explained for Newcomers

Before touching any simulation tool, make sure you genuinely understand what each of the following terms means. Click any card to expand its definition. These are not just vocabulary items — they represent the architectural decisions you will make in the lab.

🏢
HQ — HeadQuarters
HQ
The central, primary office site of an organisation. In a network context, HQ typically hosts the most critical infrastructure: main servers, core routers, Internet uplinks, and the main switching fabric. In your project, HQ is the topology you have built across all previous phases.
tap to expand ↓
🏪
Remote Branch Office
Branch
A secondary site, geographically separate from HQ, belonging to the same organisation. Branch offices are typically small — a handful of computers, a local switch, and a single router — but they must communicate reliably with HQ. Think of a bank branch: local terminals need to reach central servers constantly.
tap to expand ↓
🌐
WAN — Wide Area Network
WAN
A network spanning a large geographical area — a city, a country, or the globe. Unlike a LAN (Local Area Network), which covers a floor or building, a WAN connects multiple sites. The public Internet is the world's largest WAN. Enterprises rent dedicated WAN links from ISPs (Internet Service Providers) for guaranteed bandwidth and security.
tap to expand ↓
🖧
Network Topology
Topology
The logical and physical arrangement of devices — how routers, switches, and end devices are connected. Today you will build a hub-and-spoke topology: one central HQ ("hub") connected to a remote branch ("spoke"). This is by far the most common model for small-to-medium enterprise WAN design.
tap to expand ↓
🔀
Router
—
A device that forwards data packets between different networks based on IP (Internet Protocol) addresses and a routing table. When adding a branch, you need a router at both ends of the WAN link. These two routers must be configured to know about each other's networks — this is called routing.
tap to expand ↓
📋
Subnetting
CIDR
The process of dividing a larger IP address space into smaller logical groups called subnets. Each subnet serves a distinct part of the network (one for HQ, one for the branch, one for the WAN link itself). Good subnetting prevents IP address conflicts and keeps routing clean. A /30 subnet (255.255.255.252) gives exactly 2 usable host addresses — perfect for a point-to-point router link.
tap to expand ↓
🗺️
Static vs. Dynamic Routing
OSPF / RIP / EIGRP
Static routing: an administrator manually types every route. Simple and predictable for small networks. Dynamic routing: routers use a protocol (like OSPF — Open Shortest Path First, or RIP — Routing Information Protocol) to automatically discover and share routes. OSPF is the professional standard for enterprise networks. You will start with static routes for clarity, then optionally explore OSPF.
tap to expand ↓
🔗
Simulated WAN Link
DCE / DTE
In a real lab, a WAN link needs physical serial cables and a CSU/DSU (Channel Service Unit / Data Service Unit) device. In simulation tools, you create a virtual serial link between two routers. The DCE (Data Circuit-terminating Equipment) end provides the clock signal; the DTE (Data Terminal Equipment) end receives it. You must set a clock rate on the DCE end using the clock rate command.
tap to expand ↓
📡
Interface
GE / Serial / Fa
A physical or virtual port on a router or switch. Routers have LAN interfaces (GigabitEthernet / FastEthernet — connected to switches) and WAN interfaces (Serial — connected to another router or WAN provider). Every active interface must have an IP address and be administratively enabled using the no shutdown command in Cisco IOS (Internetwork Operating System).
tap to expand ↓
🛡️
VPN — Virtual Private Network
VPN
An encrypted tunnel over a public network (like the Internet) that allows private, secure communication between two sites. While today's lab uses an unencrypted simulated serial link, real enterprise WAN connections almost always include a VPN layer for security. Understanding the gap between today's lab and real production practice is part of your learning.
tap to expand ↓

Part 1 · ≈ 1 hour

Theory — Conceptual Grounding

1.1 Why Branch Offices Exist

Over the previous phases you have built a network that represents a single physical location — an HQ. Most real organisations, however, operate across multiple sites. A university has campuses. A bank has branches. A logistics company has depots. Each site has local users, local devices, and local services. But they all need to share data, access central systems, and communicate with each other.

The network infrastructure that ties these distributed sites together is the WAN (Wide Area Network). Understanding how to design, configure, and troubleshoot this infrastructure is a core professional competency — and it is what you are learning to do today.

1.2 WAN Technology Landscape

Real WAN connections come in several flavours. Here is a brief landscape of the most common ones:

TechnologyHow It WorksTypical UseCost
Leased LineDedicated physical connection, rented from a carrierBank branches, hospitalsHigh
MPLSProvider-managed, label-switched networkEnterprise multi-siteMedium-High
VPN over InternetEncrypted tunnel over public InternetSME, remote workersLow
Serial Point-to-PointDirect digital link — classic WAN standardLabs, legacy networksVariable
SD-WANSoftware-defined, multi-path, cloud-managedModern enterpriseMedium

In your simulation, you will use a serial point-to-point link — the classic WAN technology that has been used in network labs for decades. It is technically simple, well-understood, and perfectly suited for learning the fundamentals of routing between sites.

1.3 The Hub-and-Spoke Model

The topology you are building is called hub-and-spoke. Your HQ router is the hub — the central point that all branches connect to. Each branch is a spoke. This is by far the most common WAN design for organisations with a clear central headquarters and several secondary sites.

Its advantages include simplicity of design, centralised security enforcement, and predictable traffic patterns. Its main disadvantage is that branch-to-branch communication must always pass through HQ — which increases latency and places load on the central router. For today's scale, these are not concerns. But keep them in mind as a professional design consideration.

Professional network architects do not design in a vacuum. They follow documented standards produced by recognised international bodies:

Lab Practice — Step-by-Step

🔧 Tool Choice — Your Decision

STEP 01 Validate Your Existing HQ Topology ≈ 15 min ▶

Before adding anything new, confirm your HQ network from Phase 8 is fully functional. Adding a branch to a broken HQ produces confusing, hard-to-isolate errors. This habit is called baseline documentation — a standard practice in professional network operations (ITIL — IT Infrastructure Library).

Checklist — tick each item before proceeding:

  • All HQ devices are powered on and connected
  • IP addresses correctly assigned on all HQ interfaces
  • HQ PCs can ping each other
  • HQ PCs can reach the HQ server (if applicable)
  • HQ router routing table is clean — run show ip route
💡 Pro Tip

Save a snapshot or copy of your working Phase 8 file before making any changes. Always work on a copy. Professional engineers use version control (Git) for network configurations too.

STEP 02 Plan on Paper Before Configuring ≈ 20 min ▶

Never configure before you design. Open a diagramming tool such as draw.io or use plain paper, and produce:

  • A topology diagram showing all devices and connections
  • A complete IP addressing table (every device, every interface)
  • Your WAN link addressing (/30 subnet — identify which end is .1 and which is .2)
  • Your routing strategy: static or OSPF? Write down the exact routes you will need
📋 Report Note

Include this planning table and your topology diagram in your written report. Marks are awarded for correct, conflict-free addressing and clear logical thinking — not for complexity.

STEP 03 Build the Branch Site in Simulation ≈ 25 min ▶

In your simulation workspace, add and connect the branch devices. Place them visually apart from HQ to represent geographic distance.

  • Add a Branch Router (e.g., Cisco 2811 or 1941 in Packet Tracer)
  • Add a Branch Switch and connect to Branch Router's LAN interface
  • Add 1–3 Branch PCs and connect to Branch Switch

Configure the Branch Router's LAN interface and the Branch PCs:

Cisco IOS — Branch Router LAN Interface 
! Enter privileged mode, then global configuration
Router> enable
Router# configure terminal

! Assign IP to Branch Router's LAN interface
Router(config)# interface GigabitEthernet0/0
Router(config-if)# ip address 192.168.2.1 255.255.255.0
Router(config-if)# no shutdown
Router(config-if)# exit

! Save the configuration
Router# write memory

On each Branch PC, set the IP address within the branch subnet and set the default gateway to the Branch Router's LAN IP (192.168.2.1 in this example).

STEP 04 Create the Simulated WAN Link ≈ 20 min ▶

This step is the conceptual centrepiece of Phase 9. Connect the HQ Router's Serial interface to the Branch Router's Serial interface using a serial DCE/DTE cable. The DCE (Data Circuit-terminating Equipment) end must provide a clock rate — this synchronises the link, simulating what a real carrier would provide.

Cisco IOS — HQ Router WAN (DCE end) 
Router(config)# interface Serial0/0/0
Router(config-if)# ip address 10.0.0.1 255.255.255.252
Router(config-if)# clock rate 64000    ← DCE end only
Router(config-if)# no shutdown
Router(config-if)# exit
Cisco IOS — Branch Router WAN (DTE end) 
Router(config)# interface Serial0/0/0
Router(config-if)# ip address 10.0.0.2 255.255.255.252
! No clock rate on DTE end
Router(config-if)# no shutdown
Router(config-if)# exit
Verification — confirm WAN link is up 
Router# show interfaces Serial0/0/0
! Expected output: "Serial0/0/0 is up, line protocol is up"

Router# ping 10.0.0.1    ← from Branch Router
Router# ping 10.0.0.2    ← from HQ Router
🔁 Alternative Approach

If your tool does not support serial links, simulate the WAN using an Ethernet crossover with artificial bandwidth limits, or route traffic through a simulated ISP router in the middle. Document your design decision and explain it in your report. Research other approaches — you may find something better.

STEP 05 Configure Routing Between Sites ≈ 30 min ▶

The WAN link is physically up, but routers still do not know how to reach each other's LAN networks. You must add routing. Choose one approach — and justify your choice in the report.

Option A — Static Routing (recommended for beginners)

Static Routes — HQ Router 
! Tell HQ router: "To reach Branch LAN, send to Branch Router's WAN IP"
Router(config)# ip route 192.168.2.0 255.255.255.0 10.0.0.2
Static Routes — Branch Router 
! Tell Branch router: "To reach HQ LAN, send to HQ Router's WAN IP"
Router(config)# ip route 192.168.1.0 255.255.255.0 10.0.0.1

Option B — Dynamic Routing with OSPF (optional / advanced)

OSPF Configuration — Both Routers 
! Enable OSPF process 1 on both routers
Router(config)# router ospf 1

! HQ Router — advertise HQ LAN and WAN link into OSPF area 0
Router(config-router)# network 192.168.1.0 0.0.0.255 area 0
Router(config-router)# network 10.0.0.0 0.0.0.3 area 0

! Branch Router — advertise Branch LAN and WAN link
Router(config-router)# network 192.168.2.0 0.0.0.255 area 0
Router(config-router)# network 10.0.0.0 0.0.0.3 area 0

! Verify OSPF neighbours formed
Router# show ip ospf neighbor
Router# show ip route ospf
STEP 06 Test End-to-End Connectivity ≈ 10 min ▶

From a Branch PC, ping an HQ PC. From an HQ PC, ping a Branch PC. Both directions must work.

End-to-end connectivity test 
! From Branch PC — ping HQ PC
ping 192.168.1.10

! From HQ PC — ping Branch PC
ping 192.168.2.10

! Trace the path — you should see 3 hops
traceroute 192.168.2.10
!  1. HQ Router (10.0.0.1)
!  2. Branch Router (10.0.0.2)
!  3. Branch PC (192.168.2.10)

Troubleshooting — if ping fails:

  • First, ping the WAN endpoints only: 10.0.0.1 ↔ 10.0.0.2. If this fails, the WAN link is the problem.
  • Run show ip route on each router — check that all expected networks appear.
  • Run show interfaces — confirm no interface is administratively down.
  • Check PC default gateway settings — this is a very common mistake.
STEP 07 Optional Enrichment Challenges if time permits ▶

If you complete Steps 1–6 with time to spare, explore one or more of the following. These are not required, but completing them will deepen your understanding significantly and is highly encouraged.

  • Simulate WAN Latency: In GNS3 or EVE-NG, apply 50 ms delay to the WAN link. Observe how ping round-trip times change.
  • Add a Second Branch: Extend the topology to a third site. Configure routing so all three sites communicate.
  • Explore NAT (Network Address Translation): What happens if branch and HQ use the same IP range? Research how NAT or policy-based routing could resolve it.
  • WAN Failure Simulation: Shut down the WAN link. What breaks? Research floating static routes and dual-WAN redundancy.
  • Packet Capture: Use Wireshark to capture traffic on the WAN interface. What protocols do you see?
  • Build a Tool: Write a Python script using netmiko to automatically configure routing on both routers.

Reflection & Written Report

Your written report is not a transcript of steps you followed. It is evidence of your understanding, written for a technically competent reader who has not seen your specific design. Write critically — explain your choices, not just your actions.

📄 Maximum 4 Pages A4

Your report must not exceed four pages of A4 paper, inclusive of all diagrams and tables. Be concise and precise. The ability to communicate technical content clearly and briefly is itself a professional skill.

Report Structure

SectionExpected Content
1. IntroductionBrief context — what Phase 9 builds on, why branch expansion matters in professional practice (5–8 lines)
2. Design DecisionsYour IP addressing table, topology diagram, routing method choice with justification
3. Configuration SummaryKey commands only — not every single line. Show you understand, not that you can paste logs
4. Verification & ResultsScreenshots or simulation exports — successful pings, routing tables, traceroute output
5. ConclusionWhat worked, what didn't, what you would change in a real deployment; answer ≥ 2 reflection questions

Reflection Questions — Choose at Least Two

  • Why is the /30 subnet particularly well-suited for a point-to-point WAN link?
  • What are the security implications of connecting a remote branch to HQ over an unencrypted WAN link? What would you add to a production design?
  • What factors determine whether to use static routing or OSPF in a real enterprise scenario?
  • If your WAN link failed, how would the network behave? How would you design redundancy?
  • What is the difference between a routed WAN and a VPN WAN, and when would each be preferred?

Citing Your Sources

Use any recognised citation format consistently (APA, IEEE, or Chicago). For tools and RFCs, the URL is sufficient. Credit all sources — documentation pages, forum posts, tutorials. Academic integrity applies to networking work just as much as to written essays.
